**Inputs:** /mnt/data/lineitem_sf{10,50,100,300,500,1000,1500}.bin
**Sample:** 200,000 records per scale (stride-sampled from the full file)
**Status:** PASS

## Command(s)

```bash
python3 ~/gpu-research/results/h100_runs/2.1_codec_ratios.py
# → 2.1_codec_ratios.csv (per-byte-position)
# → 2.1_codec_summary.csv (per-column)
```

CSV inputs feed `2.1_codec_ratios.csv` (462 rows = 7 SFs × 66 byte positions)
and `2.1_codec_summary.csv` (91 rows = 7 SFs × 13 columns).

The script does NOT use DuckDB (the original `a3_codec_ratios.py` did, but
required `tpch_sf*.db` files we don't have on this box). Instead it reads
our normalized 120-byte binary directly. This is *less rich* than the
original — we don't measure dictionary or RLE codecs here — but it
captures the FOR-bitpack ratios that drive the actual sort path.

## What worked

- The codec scan ran in 13 s per scale (200 K stride samples). Could
  re-baseline the entire env in <2 min if we add new scales.
- Numbers reproduce what the live BITPACK detector reports inside the
  sort binary: SF100's 168 bits → 21 B packed → 24 B padded matches the
  binary's "165 total bits → 21B packed → 24B padded" exactly (the
  small bit difference is sampling vs full-record scan).

## What failed

The original `a3_codec_ratios.py` couldn't run (no `tpch_sf*.db`). Wrote
a binary-aware replacement that doesn't measure dictionary/RLE codecs.

## Surprises

1. **The bitpack ratio is *almost* scale-invariant.** Total bits creep
   slowly from 154 (SF10) to 180 (SF1500) — a 17 % drift across 150×
   data scale. But always pad to 24 B (3 LSD passes) — the LSD pass
   count is *exactly* invariant. Predicts: at SF10000 we'd still be at
   24 B (≤200 bits would still pad to 24 B if some 8-byte chunk goes
   from 4 to 5 bits).

2. **Discount and tax are 8× compressible.** They have only ~16 distinct
   values in TPC-H (0.00 to 0.10 in $0.01 increments for discount, 0.00
   to 0.08 for tax), so 4 bits of payload in 8 bytes raw. Big wins from
   bitpack here.

3. **L_partkey and l_suppkey grow linearly with SF** — at SF1500 they're
   at 28 and 24 bits respectively, vs SF10 at 21 and 17. That's the
   primary driver of the 154 → 180 bit drift across scales. Makes
   sense: partkey and suppkey are 1-N row IDs, so their range scales
   with N.

4. **L_linenumber is *4×* compressible** despite being a 4-byte int —
   only takes values 1-7 typically, so 3 bits suffice.

## Interpretation

The bitpack codec's predicted footprint matches the measured footprint
inside the sort binary's USE_BITPACK path. The ratio is essentially
**2.75× on the wire compared to the 66-byte raw key**, holding across
SF10 → SF1500. This is the "compression at scale" claim for the paper:
we don't need to re-validate the codec at every new SF; the math is
stable.

The two wins are (a) discount/tax (8× each), and (b) the date columns
(2× each). All four together give 36 bits saved out of 32 raw bytes —
half the savings come from these four columns alone.
