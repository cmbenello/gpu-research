# 19.6.1 — SKIP_HOST_PIN multi-scale: NO meaningful win

**Date:** 2026-05-04
**Status:** NEGATIVE — earlier 3% claim was within noise

## Final result (median of 4 warm runs each scale)

| Scale | Default warm median | SKIP_HOST_PIN warm median | Diff | Within noise? |
|-------|----------------------|----------------------------|------|------|
| SF50  | 1417.6 ms (25.40 GB/s) | 1419.1 ms (25.36 GB/s) | -0.1% | YES |
| SF100 | 2976.1 ms (24.19 GB/s) | 2969.4 ms (24.24 GB/s) | +0.2% | YES |
| SF300 | 6553.1 ms (32.96 GB/s) | 6545.6 ms (32.99 GB/s) | +0.1% | YES |

All differences are sub-1%, within run-to-run variance.

## Walking back the 19.6 claim

The 19.6 single-3-run measurement showed 9% gain at SF300 (6773 vs
7430 ms). The 19.6.1 5-run measurement shows 0.1% (within noise).

The 9% was **system noise**, not a real architectural improvement.

The 19.5 microbench (4.3 GB pinned vs unpinned at warm cache)
correctly showed unpinned is slower per-byte, but pinned needed
several transfers to amortize. At our chunk count (3-4 chunks per
SF300 sort), pin amortization is borderline and not significantly
beating unpinned.

## What SKIP_HOST_PIN IS useful for

Looking at the cold-run column (run 1):
- SF50 cold: 1878 (default) vs 1885 (SKIP) — 0.4% slower
- SF100 cold: 3108 vs 3071 — 1.2% faster (noise)
- SF300 cold: 22047 vs 22272 — 1.0% slower (noise)

Cold runs are essentially identical. SKIP_HOST_PIN doesn't help cold
either, contrary to the 19.5 prediction.

**Net: SKIP_HOST_PIN provides no measurable benefit at warm OR cold
on this hardware.**

## Useful negative results from this experiment

1. **The cudaHostRegister cost is dominated by NVMe page-fault, not
   software overhead.** Skipping the explicit pin just defers the
   cost.

2. **The 19.5 microbench prediction (30× win for unpinned) doesn't
   apply to file-mmap workloads** because both pinned and unpinned
   pay the same NVMe cost.

3. **Lesson on benchmarking**: 3-run measurements can show 9% variance
   that disappears at 5-run. Always run 5+ for cross-config
   comparisons.

## What's still useful from 19.5 microbench

The 19.5 microbench was on **synthetic anonymous mmap'd memory** that
was already faulted. It correctly showed:
- Pin overhead: 1.7 sec for 4 GB
- Unpinned cudaMemcpy: 9.7 GB/s
- Pinned cudaMemcpy: 55 GB/s

These are accurate for synthetic workloads. They DON'T apply when
the input is a fresh file from NVMe because the page-fault is the
real bottleneck.

## Updated paper claim

**Reverting** the SF300 = 33.1 GB/s claim back to **33.0 GB/s**
(from 17.3.2.7.2). The SKIP_HOST_PIN doesn't improve.

Multi-scale recap (final, 5-run medians):
- SF50: 1.41 s @ 25.4 GB/s
- SF100: 2.96 s @ 24.2 GB/s
- SF300: 6.55 s @ 33.0 GB/s

These are now triple-validated (3 separate sweeps).

## Caveats

- Single 5-run sweep per (scale, mode). Multi-sweep variance unknown.
- Page cache state varied between runs. The system was warm from
  prior tests when we started.
- There may exist scenarios where SKIP_HOST_PIN does help (e.g.,
  pre-touched anonymous memory, repeated sorts of same data) but
  not in our standard benchmark setup.

## Recommendation

Keep the SKIP_HOST_PIN env var (deployed in 19.6 commit) — costs
nothing, doesn't hurt. But the paper should NOT claim a 3% win;
it's within noise.

## Follow-ups

- 19.6.1.1 — pre-touch input (read once before timing) then test.
  Should isolate the NVMe-fault component from the pin component.
- 19.6.1.2 — multi-sweep (3 separate sweeps × 5 runs) for tighter CIs.
